Tools and Distribution Models for Games on the Modern Web

Angelina Fabbro and Bill Maggs, Mozilla @ GDC 2014
"Hi, I'm Bill Maggs, I work on game products at Mozilla. In the first two talks today, we heard about how technologies like asm.js will provide native games a home on the Web; we talked a lot about the Web as a target for game developrment. Now we will concentrate on the Web as an ENVIRONMENT in which to troublshoot and optimize your game and get it to market, with human-written rather than machine-generated Javascript the main ingredient. We hope to get you some You've seen the future; now using the example of JS games, we'll talk about the present, with the help of some guests that are all in on the Web for games. We think you might be surprised at the state of the art for tools and engines for JS games, and at what developers are finding when they take their games to the Web."

".Then, boom, the Cambrian explosion, an amazing period of evolutionary experimentation in the fossil record, with many wonderful and strange designs by nature: In the world of JS games, many engines evolved, also with innovative and sometimes strange designs"
"Today the same thing is happening as did to animals in the geologic past: some design patterns are winning. As game developers you have always asked some basic questions about building for the Web: Where’s the toolchain? Wheres the workflow? At Mozilla we're not interested in replacing your existing tools, but rather augmenting the way you work now. Here to talk about that is Angelina Fabbro from the Mozilla Developer Tools team.""
"It's not an accident or coincidence that we were recently featured on PRWire in Times Square for our developer tools. This photo was taken during Mobile World Congress, where we also had some big annoucements just like this week." "We've seen today that it's both feasible and promising to port your game to the web with asm.js, but what happens when your game lands on the web? How do you debug any cross-platform issues in an efficient manner? Further, here we are talking about porting your game from another language - what about JavaScript developers that are making games on the web in their browser as it's first platform? At Mozilla, we've been working on providing tools to suit both category of developer, and noting that often the needs of the two overlap."

But... Browsers?

"The first thing you're going to notice is that your game ends up in the context of a document object model that comprises what we colloquially refer to as a 'webpage' and often when there is more sophisticated behavior, an 'application'. One of the interesting things about working on the web is the control over your environment. Because you have control over the detail of the envinronment that you've embedded your game, you can style it and also program it in compelling ways to provide auxillary content to your users, whether it be adds or additional social features."
